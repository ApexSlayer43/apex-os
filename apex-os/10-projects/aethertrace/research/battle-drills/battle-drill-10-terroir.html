01 Philosophy

The field and the loom are the same gesture

Growing food and weaving cloth are both acts of patience applied to raw material. Both depend on soil, season, and the willingness to wait. We built Terroir & Thread because we couldn't find a restaurant that understood this. So we started a farm. Then we started a loom house. Then we opened the doors between them.

Every piece of textile in our dining room — the napkins, the table runners, the wall hangings — is woven from flax and wool raised on our property or sourced from farms we can drive to. Every dish is built from ingredients harvested within the same week you eat them. Nothing is decorative. Everything is connected.

This is not farm-to-table as a marketing phrase. It is farm-to-table as a material practice — one that extends from the dirt under the carrots to the thread in the cloth that holds them.

The loom house is not a gift shop. It is the other half of the restaurant.

We raise flax and keep a small flock of sheep — both for the fiber. Our weaver, Ingrid Solberg, operates a 200-year-old floor loom in the barn adjacent to the kitchen. The napkin under your plate was woven within walking distance of where you're sitting.

Guests are welcome to visit the loom house before or after dinner. We hold monthly weaving workshops for those who want to understand the process — from raw fleece to finished cloth. Every textile is available for purchase.
